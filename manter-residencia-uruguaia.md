Uruguay has become one of the preferred destinations for people who want to live, invest or do business outside their home country. Institutional stability, legal certainty and a solid financial system explain much of that movement.

But there is a long way between *wanting* to live in Uruguay and *structuring* the move correctly. This guide summarizes the essentials before you take the first step.

## Legal residency and tax residency are not the same thing

This is the most common mistake — and the most expensive one.

**Legal (immigration) residency** is the authorization to live in the country: it lets you reside, work and access services in Uruguay.

**Tax residency** is a different discussion: it determines in which country you are taxed. It follows its own criteria, such as the number of days spent in the country during the year (the classic reference is 183 days), the center of vital or economic interests and, in certain cases, qualifying investments in the country.

In practice: it is perfectly possible to hold legal residency in Uruguay while remaining a tax resident of your home country — and vice versa. If your goal involves tax organization, both tracks must be planned together, including the formal tax exit from your home country where applicable.

## The most common paths

Nationals of Mercosur countries have an important advantage: the bloc's residence agreements, which significantly simplify the immigration process.

Broadly, the most frequent paths are:

| Path | Who it usually suits |
| --- | --- |
| Residency via the Mercosur agreement | Nationals of the bloc — the most direct route |
| Residency based on proof of income | Retirees and people living off income |
| Residency combined with investment | Those structuring wealth and business in the country |

Which is best for you? It depends on your goal (living, investing, doing business), your family situation and the intended tax design. That is exactly what a proper diagnosis defines before any filing.

## The typical stages of the process

Every case has its particulars, but the backbone is usually:

1. **Diagnosis and strategy** — defining the migration path and the tax design before touching any document.
2. **Document preparation** — certificates, apostilles and translations in the right order (documents expire; sequence matters).
3. **Filing in Uruguay** — submitting the application and starting the procedure with the competent authorities.
4. **Follow-through to completion** — the process has its own stages and timelines; documentation errors are the main cause of delays.
5. **Post-approval** — the local ID document and, when part of the plan, structuring tax residency and the other fronts (accounts, company, property).

## The mistakes that cost the most

- **Treating the move as a procedure rather than a project.** Residency, taxes, accounts and wealth are parts of the same design.
- **Copying someone else's structure.** What worked for a friend may be inadequate — or risky — for your case.
- **Ignoring the tax exit from your home country.** Without it, you may end up with obligations (and taxation) in two countries.
- **Leaving documents for the last minute.** Certificates expire and apostilles take time; the wrong order creates rework.
